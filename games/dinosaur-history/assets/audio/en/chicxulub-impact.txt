Sixty-six million years ago, an object about fourteen kilometers wide struck near today’s Yucatán Peninsula. The impact formed the Chicxulub crater—pronounced CHEEK-shuh-loob—about one hundred eighty kilometers across. The blast was terrible, but the extinction was not one instant when every animal fell. Dust and debris dimmed sunlight. Plants struggled. Food webs broke down across land and sea. About three quarters of species disappeared. Strong evidence identifies the impact as the main trigger, while enormous volcanic eruptions were also changing the climate. Every non-bird dinosaur lineage ended. Yet one small feathered branch made it through: early birds, carrying the dinosaur story beyond the Cretaceous.
