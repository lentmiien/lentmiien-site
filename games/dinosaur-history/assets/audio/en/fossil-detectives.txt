How can anyone know a world that no human saw? Paleontologists start with evidence in rock. Bones and teeth are body fossils. Footprints, nests, skin impressions, and fossilized dung are traces of activity. A trackway can preserve direction and stride at one real moment. Teeth can hold scratches from meals. Thin slices of bone can show growth lines. Complete skeletons are rare, and soft tissues are rarer still. Most dinosaur colors and sounds remain unknown, although a few exceptional feathers preserve tiny pigment clues. Scientists compare fossils with geology, physics, and living animals. Paleoartists then build a visible idea—part evidence, part clearly marked uncertainty.
